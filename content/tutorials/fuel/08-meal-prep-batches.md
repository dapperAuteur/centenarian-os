## Narrator Script

Meal prep is one of the most effective strategies for eating consistently well. Cook in bulk on Sunday, and healthy food is ready to grab for the rest of the week. The Meal Prep module tracks your batches — what you made, when, how many servings, and how many are left.

---

### What a Meal Prep Batch Is

A meal prep batch ties a protocol to a specific cooking session. Instead of logging a single serving of your "Chicken and Roasted Vegetables" protocol, you record that you cooked eight servings on Sunday. The batch tracks how many servings remain. Each time you log a meal from that batch, the count goes down.

This is different from logging individual meals. Batch tracking answers: *how much of this do I have left before I need to cook again?*

---

### Navigating to Meal Prep

Go to `/dashboard/fuel/meal-prep`. You'll see your batches — active batches (still have servings remaining) and finished batches (empty or marked done).

The Fuel dashboard also shows an **Active Batches** count so you can see at a glance how many prepared meals you have waiting.

---

### Starting a New Batch

Click **New Batch**.

**Protocol** — Which protocol did you cook? Select from your library. The batch is linked to that protocol, so its nutrition data (calories, NCV score, cost) applies to each serving.

**Date made** — When you cooked it. Defaults to today.

**Servings made** — How many portions did you make? This is the number you'll eat across the week. If you cooked a big pot of soup that yields 6 portions, enter 6.

**Storage location** (optional) — Where it's stored: Fridge, Freezer, Pantry, Work fridge. Useful when you prep multiple batches and want to know where to look.

**Notes** (optional) — Any variations from the standard protocol. "Added extra garlic", "Used sweet potato instead of regular", "Portioned into meal prep containers."

Click **Save**. The batch appears as active with its servings count.

---

### Tracking Servings

Each batch shows how many servings remain out of how many you made. When you eat a portion, you mark it:

Click the **Consume Serving** button (or the − icon) on the batch card. The count goes down by one.

When servings reach zero, the batch is automatically marked as finished. It moves from the Active view to the Finished view.

You can also manually click **Mark as Finished** on a batch if you finished it, gave some away, or the batch went bad.

---

### The Relationship Between Batches and Meal Logs

Meal prep batches and the meal log work together but are separate records:

- The **batch** tracks how much food you have prepared
- The **meal log** tracks what you ate and when

When you log a meal from a batch, you're recording the consumption in your meal history. The batch tracking is separate — you update the serving count when you eat from it.

This does mean two taps instead of one: mark a serving consumed in the batch, and log the meal in your meal log. Some users do both; others only do one or the other depending on which data they care about more.

---

### Filtering Active vs. Finished Batches

Use the filter tabs at the top of the Meal Prep page:

**Active** — Batches with servings remaining. This is your default view — what's in the fridge right now.

**Finished** — Completed batches. Your prep history. Useful for seeing how often you batch-cook, which protocols you make most often, and roughly how long each batch lasts.

---

### Batch Size and Planning

How big should your batches be? A useful rule of thumb: prep enough for 3–5 days of a given meal. More than that risks food going bad before you eat it; less than that defeats the purpose of batch cooking.

For most people, a Sunday prep session with 2–3 batches (lunch, dinner, snacks) covers a full week. The Fuel dashboard's Active Batches count helps you know whether you're running low on prepared food before the week ends.

---

### A Practical Workflow

Here's a typical batch-cooking weekend workflow in CentenarianOS:

1. Check **Inventory** for low stock — go shopping if needed
2. Check **Recipe Ideas** for inspiration from what you have
3. Cook 2–3 protocols in bulk
4. Create a **New Batch** for each one, entering servings made
5. Throughout the week: mark servings consumed as you eat
6. Log individual meals in the **Meal Log** if you want detailed nutrition tracking
7. Repeat next weekend

Once the habit is set, steps 4–5 take about two minutes per batch.

## Key Takeaways

- A meal prep batch records a bulk cooking session: protocol + date + servings made
- Track servings consumed by clicking the Consume Serving button — count depletes until the batch is finished
- Active batches are shown on the Fuel dashboard; filter Active vs. Finished in the Meal Prep view
- Batches and Meal Logs are separate — batches track quantity; meal logs track what you ate and when
- Storage location notes tell you where to find each batch in your fridge/freezer
- Prep 3–5 days' worth per batch; aim for 2–3 batches per prep session to cover a full week
