## Narrator Script

Before you start adding ingredients and building protocols, you need to understand the NCV framework. It's a three-tier scoring system that classifies food by how much nutritional value it delivers per calorie — and it's the foundation of everything the Fuel module shows you.

---

### What NCV Stands For

NCV stands for **Nutrient vs. Calorie Value**.

The name says it all: it's a ratio of nutritional benefit to caloric cost. Food that packs a lot of vitamins, minerals, fiber, and protein into a relatively small calorie load scores high. Food that delivers primarily calories with little nutritional complexity scores low.

This is sometimes called "nutrient density" in nutrition science. The NCV framework translates that concept into a simple three-level color system you can apply to every ingredient and meal.

---

### The Three Tiers

**Green — High nutrient density**

These are foods where the nutritional value is high relative to the caloric load. Think leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, berries, legumes, fish, eggs, whole grains, nuts, and seeds. When you eat Green foods, you're getting a lot for each calorie.

In the Fuel module, Green-scored ingredients and protocols are the target. A meal with a high proportion of Green ingredients gets a Green NCV score overall.

**Yellow — Moderate nutrient density**

These foods provide a reasonable mix of nutrition and calories. Starchy vegetables, whole grain bread, lean meats, dairy products, and many whole foods fall here. They're not as nutrient-dense per calorie as Green foods, but they're a normal, healthy part of eating.

A diet with lots of Yellow and some Green is perfectly healthy — Yellow foods are not a warning, just a marker that the item is less nutrient-dense than Green-tier foods.

**Red — Low nutrient density / high caloric load**

Red foods are calorie-dense with relatively low nutritional return: refined sugars, processed snacks, white flour products, fried foods, candy, and most fast food. Eating Red foods occasionally is fine — the concern is when they dominate your intake.

The Fuel module doesn't restrict or block Red foods. It just makes them visible so you can make informed decisions. If you're eating mostly Green and Yellow with occasional Red, you're doing well.

---

### How NCV Scores Are Calculated

When you add an ingredient to your library, you assign it a Green, Yellow, or Red score based on its nutritional profile. The system doesn't calculate this automatically — you make the call based on your understanding of the food.

A rough guide:
- If the food is mostly vegetables, legumes, fruit, fish, or eggs → **Green**
- If it's a whole grain product, lean protein, dairy, or starchy vegetable → **Yellow**
- If it's a processed snack, sugar, refined grain, or fried food → **Red**

When you create a protocol (a meal template), the system calculates an overall NCV score based on the calorie-weighted average of all the ingredients in that meal. A smoothie with mostly Green ingredients gets a Green protocol score. A pasta dish with a red-scored sauce and a green salad might average out to Yellow.

---

### Why NCV Instead of Macros?

Macro tracking (calories, protein, carbs, fat) is useful but incomplete. Two foods can have identical macros and wildly different health impacts because of what the numbers don't capture: micronutrients, fiber, phytonutrients, inflammatory load.

NCV is a simplification, but it points in the right direction. The goal isn't perfection — it's a system that makes "mostly nutrient-dense food" the path of least resistance. If your weekly Fuel dashboard shows a consistently Green NCV average, you're eating well, regardless of whether you hit some macro target.

Longevity research consistently shows that diet quality matters more than any specific nutrient ratio. NCV is built to track quality.

---

### NCV in Practice

Once you're up and running, you'll see NCV scores everywhere in the module:
- Ingredient cards show a Green, Yellow, or Red badge
- Protocol cards show the overall NCV score for that meal
- The Fuel dashboard shows your average NCV score for the week

Over time, you can track whether your diet is trending greener. Most people find that once they start seeing the score, they gradually shift their ingredient choices without any conscious effort — the feedback loop does the work.

## Key Takeaways

- NCV = Nutrient vs. Calorie Value — a measure of food quality, not just quantity
- **Green** = high nutrient density (vegetables, legumes, fish, eggs, whole foods)
- **Yellow** = moderate (lean proteins, dairy, starchy vegetables, whole grains)
- **Red** = low nutrient density / calorie-dense (processed foods, sugar, refined grains)
- You assign NCV scores when adding ingredients; protocols calculate a weighted average automatically
- The goal: weekly dashboard showing a consistently Green average NCV score
- No foods are restricted — NCV makes diet quality visible so you can make informed choices
