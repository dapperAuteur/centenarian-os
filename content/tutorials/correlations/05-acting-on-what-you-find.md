## Narrator Script

Insight without action is just interesting data. This lesson covers how to take the patterns you find in Correlations and Analytics and turn them into real protocol changes — systematically, not impulsively.

---

### The Two Types of Findings

**Confirming findings** — the correlation matches what you'd expect and what the research says. Sleep correlates with energy. Green meals correlate with higher energy. High pain reduces focus.

These are useful for two reasons: they validate that your logging is accurate (the data is behaving as biology predicts), and they give you quantified motivation. "I know I should sleep more" is easier to ignore than "My data shows that days under 7 hours have 31% lower energy ratings — here's the scatter plot."

**Surprising findings** — the correlation is unexpected, missing, or reversed. Sleep and energy barely correlate. Green meals are uncorrelated with energy. High focus days don't improve task completion.

These are where the real discoveries live. A weak sleep↔energy correlation when you'd expect a strong one points to something else being the limiting factor — maybe sleep quality (not hours), or something post-sleep like meal timing or activity load. A missing focus↔completion correlation might mean your focus sessions are on low-priority tasks.

---

### The Protocol Change Framework

When you find a pattern worth acting on, use this process:

**Step 1: Verify the sample size.** A finding at 8 days is not reliable enough to change your behavior. Wait until you have 20+ days before making protocol changes based on a correlation.

**Step 2: Identify the most actionable variable.** For a sleep↔energy correlation, you can control sleep hours — so that's the variable to change. For a pain↔focus correlation, the actionable variable is pain management, not focus technique.

**Step 3: Make one change at a time.** Change multiple variables simultaneously and you won't know which one drove the outcome. Choose the single highest-impact variable and change it deliberately for 4–6 weeks.

**Step 4: Re-check after 30 more days.** Return to the Correlations page after your protocol change. Did the correlation strengthen? Did the effect size in Analytics increase? This is how you validate that your intervention worked.

**Step 5: Update your protocols.** If the change produced the expected result in the data, codify it into a protocol: a new nutrition rule, a new sleep policy, a training load adjustment.

---

### Practical Examples

**Finding:** Sleep Hours ↔ Energy Rating shows r = +0.71 at 30 days. "Strong." AI insight: "Days with 7+ hours average 4.1/5 energy vs 2.8/5 on under-7 hours."

**Action:** Set a hard sleep target: in bed by 10:30 PM every night for the next 30 days. Log it consistently. Return to Correlations at day 30 and check if the scatter plot has tightened and the average energy on 7+ hour nights has increased.

---

**Finding:** Green Meal % ↔ Energy shows r = +0.28 at 30 days. "Weak." Analytics shows 65% confidence.

**Action:** Don't change anything yet. This isn't strong enough to act on. Wait until you have more data (60+ days) and see if the coefficient strengthens. If it stays weak, nutrition quality may not be the primary energy driver for you — investigate sleep quality or training load instead.

---

**Finding:** Analytics shows "High Pain (4+) → Reduced Focus: 28% less focused work on high-pain days" with 82% confidence.

**Action:** Start the corrective exercise protocol from your CES assessment. Log pain intensity consistently. Return in 60 days and check whether the pain log average has dropped and whether the correlation card's impact figure has decreased.

---

### What Not to Do

**Don't over-act on weak correlations.** Gray "Weak" cards and low-confidence Analytics findings are noise until proven otherwise.

**Don't change multiple variables at once.** If you improve sleep, switch to all-green meals, and start a corrective exercise program simultaneously, you won't know what moved the needle.

**Don't ignore expected correlations.** If sleep and energy are strongly correlated in your data, treat that as the most important finding on the page — not the least interesting because it's "obvious."

**Don't stop logging.** The data is only as good as the consistency of what goes in. A week of gaps in health metrics or meal logging creates missing data points that weaken every correlation on the page.

## Key Takeaways

- Confirming findings validate your data quality; surprising or missing findings are where discoveries are
- Protocol change framework: verify sample size (≥20 days) → identify actionable variable → one change at a time → re-check after 30 days → codify if validated
- Don't act on Weak correlations or <50% confidence findings — wait for more data
- Don't change multiple variables simultaneously — you won't know what worked
- The Coach is a natural companion to Correlations: bring your findings to a coaching session to develop protocols
- Consistency of logging is the foundation — gaps weaken every correlation on the page
