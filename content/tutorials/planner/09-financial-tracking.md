## Narrator Script

Goals cost money. Training equipment, coaching fees, software, books, course fees, therapy, supplements, recording equipment — pursuing meaningful goals requires real investment. CentenarianOS lets you attach financial data to every level of your plan so you can see the true cost of your goals and track the return on your investments.

---

### Financial Fields at Every Level

Every roadmap, goal, milestone, and task has three financial fields:

**Estimated cost** — What you expect to spend. Before you start, estimate the cost: a training plan might cost $50/month, a music course might cost $200, a race entry might cost $100. Estimated costs sum up through the hierarchy so you can see the projected financial commitment of a whole goal or roadmap.

**Actual cost** — What you actually spent. Update this as costs occur. This might be the same as estimated (if you planned well) or different (costs always run over).

**Revenue** — Income generated from this work. If you're building a freelance skill, a business, or a creative output you sell, log the revenue here. A task might represent a client meeting, a sale, or a launch. Logging revenue lets you calculate the return on your investment.

The difference between revenue and actual cost is your **net profit** (or net investment, if it's negative). Seeing "$2,400 invested in this goal, $0 returned so far" is honest feedback. Seeing "$800 invested, $3,200 returned" is motivating.

---

### Where Financial Data Shows Up

**Task cards in the Daily Planner** — if a task has a cost or revenue entry, it shows inline in the card: green "+$X" for revenue, red "-$X" for cost. These are quick-scan reminders of the financial dimension of your daily work.

**Planner summary bar** — at the bottom of the planner (or in a summary card), the current view period's financial totals are shown: Estimated Cost, Actual Cost, Revenue, and Net Profit. For a week view, this shows the week's financial picture. For a month view, the month's.

**Roadmap hierarchy** — financial totals flow upward. A goal's total cost is the sum of all its milestones' costs, which are the sum of their tasks' costs. You can see at the goal or roadmap level what the entire project costs and earns.

---

### Practical Uses

**Project tracking** — If a milestone is "Launch a product" and the tasks include "hire a designer ($500)", "run ads ($200)", "set up hosting ($50)", the milestone's actual cost reflects the true launch spend.

**ROI on learning** — If you invest $1,000 in courses and coaching to learn a skill, and that skill generates $5,000 in freelance revenue over the next year, you can see that 5x return in your plan.

**Longevity investment tracking** — Some users track health spending here: gym membership, supplements, coaching fees. Seeing the annual investment in your physical health written down is useful context for whether you're actually prioritizing it.

---

### Keeping It Simple

Financial tracking is optional at every level. Many users only track costs and revenue for professional or business goals and leave personal development goals without financial data. That's fine — the system only surfaces financial information when it's been entered.

If financial tracking adds friction to your planning workflow, skip it entirely. The core value of the Planner is the hierarchy and the daily execution loop. Financials are an enrichment layer, not a requirement.

## Key Takeaways

- Every roadmap, goal, milestone, and task has: Estimated cost, Actual cost, Revenue
- Financial data is optional — add it when it's relevant, skip it when it's not
- Task cards show inline cost (-$X in red) and revenue (+$X in green) at a glance
- The Planner summary bar shows total cost, revenue, and net profit for the current view period
- Costs aggregate upward — a goal's total = sum of all its milestones, which = sum of their tasks
- Useful for: project economics, ROI on learning, and tracking your investment in health and longevity
