## Narrator Script

Longevity isn't just about what you can do — it's about avoiding the injuries and accumulated damage that limit what you can do in the future. The Pain Log is where you track body signals: discomfort, soreness, acute pain, chronic patterns. Done consistently, it becomes one of the most useful datasets in the system.

---

### Why Log Pain

Most people ignore minor pain until it becomes a real injury. By that point, there was usually a warning period — a few weeks of minor signals that got dismissed. The Pain Log is where those signals go.

A single pain entry is just data. Ten entries over three weeks showing the same pattern — left knee, moderate intensity, after running — is actionable information. You can see it, name it, and address it before it becomes a forced rest.

The pain data also feeds into the weekly review. If your energy was consistently low and your pain levels were elevated, Gemini can note the correlation and suggest recovery as the priority for the coming week.

### Logging a Pain Entry

From the Engine dashboard, navigate to **Pain Log** or click **+ Log Pain** if visible.

The pain entry form has:

---

### Pain Form Fields

**Date** — The calendar date this pain occurred or was first noticed. Defaults to today.

**Intensity** — A 1–10 scale.
- 1–2: Mild, barely noticeable. Slight soreness or stiffness.
- 3–4: Moderate. Present but not limiting activity.
- 5–6: Significant. Affects your range of motion or performance.
- 7–8: Severe. Difficult to train through. Likely modifying activities.
- 9–10: Extreme. Can't perform normal activities. See a professional.

**Body location** — Where is the pain? Twelve options cover the major areas:
- Head / Neck
- Left shoulder / Right shoulder
- Left elbow / Right elbow
- Left wrist / Right wrist
- Lower back / Upper back
- Left hip / Right hip
- Left knee / Right knee
- Left ankle / Right ankle
- Left foot / Right foot
- Abdomen / Core
- Chest
- Other

**Sensation type** — What does it feel like? Five options:
- **Sharp** — acute, localized, stabbing sensation
- **Dull** — diffuse, aching, low-grade discomfort
- **Burning** — heat, friction, nerve sensation
- **Throbbing** — pulsing, pressure, often inflammation
- **Stiffness** — reduced range of motion, tension without acute pain

**Activity that triggered it** (optional) — What were you doing when you noticed it? "Running", "heavy squat session", "seated at desk all day", "woke up with it." This field is the most useful for pattern recognition.

**Notes** (optional) — Any additional context: how long it's been present, whether it's getting better or worse, what helps.

---

### Multiple Locations

You can log multiple pain entries per day — one per location. If your left knee and lower back are both bothering you, log two entries with the same date. Each entry is tracked independently so trends by location are accurate.

---

### Viewing Pain History

From the Pain Log section, you can view your entries sorted by date. Filter by body location to see the history of a specific area — for example, all left knee entries over the past three months.

The history view shows intensity over time as a simple chart per location. Upward trends are warning signs. Downward trends mean whatever you're doing is working.

---

### When to Log

Log pain the same day it appears, not days later. Intensity ratings especially lose accuracy with time — what you remember as a 6 might have been a 4 at the time.

It doesn't need to be every minor ache. Use it when:
- Pain is noticeable enough to affect your workout, focus, or daily movement
- You've had the same discomfort two or more times
- You want a record to show a physical therapist or doctor ("here's the pattern over the last 6 weeks")

## Key Takeaways

- Pain Log captures: date, intensity (1–10), body location (12 zones), sensation (5 types), trigger activity, notes
- Log the same day — intensity ratings lose accuracy with time
- Multiple entries per day are fine — one per location
- Filter history by body location to see trends for a specific area
- Pain data feeds into the AI weekly review: repeated high-intensity entries trigger recovery recommendations
- Use it for noticeable pain, recurring discomfort, or when you need a record to share with a professional
