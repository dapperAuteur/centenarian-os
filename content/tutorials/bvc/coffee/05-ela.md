# English Language Arts — Stories in Every Cup

*Better Vice Club Episode 1 · Segment 4 · Approximate listening time: 8 minutes*

Every word we use for coffee came from somewhere. Every cup is wrapped in stories. This segment treats language, literature, and media literacy as tools for reading the world through your morning ritual.

## The word "coffee" traced through four languages

| English | Origin language | Original word | Pronunciation | Route |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Coffee | Arabic → Turkish → Italian → English | qahwa → kahve → caffè | KAH-wah → KAH-veh → kah-FEH | Arabic originally meant "wine" |
| Buna | Amharic (Ethiopian) | buna | BOO-nah | Coffee AND the ceremony |
| Café | French (from Italian) | caffè | kah-FAY | Both drink and place |
| Espresso | Italian | esprimere | ess-PRESS-oh | "To press out" |
| Barista | Italian | barista | bah-REE-stah | "Bartender" |
| Mocha | Arabic (place name) | Al-Mukha | al-MUK-hah | Yemeni port city |

*Source: Weinberg & Bealer (2001).*

**What the trail reveals:** trade routes shaped vocabulary. Ethiopian origin → Yemeni trade port → Ottoman Empire → Mediterranean commerce → European colonialism → global English. Each linguistic transformation marks a cultural and economic exchange.

## The Ethiopian Coffee Ceremony as three-act narrative

The Ethiopian **Buna** ceremony follows a three-round structure that mirrors classical storytelling:

1. **Abol** (first round) — strongest brew. Initial encounter, intensity. *Rising action.*
2. **Tona** (second round) — medium strength. Deepening relationship, familiarity. *Development.*
3. **Baraka** (third round) — lightest brew. Blessing, departure, peace. *Resolution.*

The ceremony **is** a narrative form. Each round tells a different part of the story of the gathering (Pankhurst, 1997).

## Primary sources for analysis

See the documents panel for full-text versions of:

1. **The Women's Petition Against Coffee (1674)** — satirical broadside. Demonstrates: persuasive rhetoric, gendered argument, satirical voice, audience awareness.
2. **The Men's Answer to the Women's Petition (1674)** — counter-broadside. Demonstrates: rebuttal technique, humor as rhetorical tool, defense of public spaces.
3. **Balzac, "The Pleasures and Pains of Coffee" (1830)** — personal essay on extreme coffee consumption. Demonstrates: first-person narrative, obsessive voice, creative-process documentation.
4. **Colonial coffee plantation records (18th c.)** — primary source business documents. Demonstrate: economic rhetoric, dehumanizing language in labor records.

Archive access: British Library Digital Collections, Library of Congress.

## Media literacy — reading coffee ads

When analyzing a coffee advertisement, examine:

1. **Who is shown?** Demographics, lifestyle, economic class.
2. **Who is absent?** Workers, farmers, shift workers, elderly, disabled.
3. **What story is being told?** Leisure vs. labor. Luxury vs. necessity.
4. **What techniques are used?** Emotional appeal, lifestyle association, aspiration.
5. **What's the gap between ad narrative and consumption data?**

Reality check: the highest coffee consumers are adults working **40+ hours/week** (SCA, 2024). Most ads show leisure — sunlit cafés, unhurried moments. The gap between what's sold and who buys reveals how texts are shaped by purpose and audience.

## Interview ethics (for student projects)

If you interview people about their coffee culture:

1. **Informed consent** — explain project and recording use upfront
2. **Right to review** — offer interviewees the chance to see how their words are used
3. **Respectful representation** — you're a guest in someone's story
4. **Cultural sensitivity** — coffee rituals carry deep personal and cultural meaning; approach with curiosity, not judgment
5. **Attribution** — credit sources by name (with permission) or respectful description

Stories are how we process the world. Your morning coffee carries stories — yours, your community's, and ones from five centuries of encounter, commerce, and translation. Next time you sit down with a cup, notice which story you're telling.
