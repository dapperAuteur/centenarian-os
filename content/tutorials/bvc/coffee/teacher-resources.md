## Activity 1 — Coffee Economics in My Cup *(25 min, in-class)*

**Materials:** calculator, Data Sheet C from Lesson 4, handout with pricing grid.

**Instructions:**

1. Pick one coffee you bought recently (home-brew, café, chain — anything)
2. Work out: what did you pay? What fraction went to the farmer under commodity system? Under direct trade?
3. Redo the math for a $4 gas-station coffee vs. a $6 local-roaster coffee
4. Class discussion: when does paying more for coffee make economic sense? When doesn't it?

**Debrief prompts:** How would your answer change if you were a small-café owner? A chain shareholder? A farmer?

## Activity 2 — Map the Bean *(30 min, in-class)*

**Materials:** blank world map (printable — attached), colored pencils or markers, Data Sheet A from Lesson 2.

**Instructions:**

1. Mark the Coffee Belt boundaries (25°N to 30°S) on the map
2. Plot the top 10 producing countries and shade by production volume (darker = more)
3. For three countries of your choice, annotate: elevation range, primary coffee type, annual rainfall
4. Draw arrows showing the three largest historical coffee trade routes (producer → consumer)
5. Mark areas projected to lose coffee viability by 2050 (use Bunn et al., 2015 data)

**Extension:** Using Google Earth, find satellite imagery of coffee farms in two different countries. Compare landscape, elevation, and surrounding environment. Write 3 sentences on how geography influences the coffee differently.

## Activity 3 — The Coffeehouse Debate *(45 min, in-class)*

**Materials:** primary-source excerpts (Royal Proclamation 1675, Women's/Men's Petition 1674) from Data Sheet B.

**Setup (10 min):** Split class into four groups.

- **Group 1:** 17th-century London coffeehouse supporters — argue for open public debate spaces
- **Group 2:** King Charles II's government — argue coffeehouses are dangerous to social order
- **Group 3:** Modern fair-trade advocates — argue for economic justice in coffee trade
- **Group 4:** Commodity-market defenders — argue the current system is efficient and feeds global demand

**Preparation (15 min):** Each group uses Data Sheet B + Social Studies segment to build their argument with ≥3 pieces of historical or economic evidence.

**Debate (15 min):**
- Round 1: each group's 2-minute opening argument
- Round 2: cross-examination — groups question each other (1 min per exchange)
- Round 3: 1-min closing statements

**Debrief (5 min):** Which arguments were strongest? Where did historical parallels appear between 17th-century and modern debates? What has changed, and what hasn't?

## Activity 4 — Advertisement Deconstruction Lab *(45 min, in-class)*

**Materials:** print or screen captures of 3–4 coffee ads (Starbucks, independent roaster, fair-trade brand, gas-station chain), Media Literacy framework from Lesson 5.

**Instructions:**

1. For each ad, answer the 5-question framework: Who's shown? Who's absent? What story? What techniques? What's the gap between ad and consumption data?
2. Rank the ads from most honest to most aspirational
3. Write a 150-word analysis of the most-aspirational ad: what is it really selling (beyond coffee)?
4. Group share-out: find patterns across ads — what's common?

## Knowledge-check answers

Student quiz questions in Lesson 7 are multiple-choice. Two essay-style questions appear in the Lesson 8 project reflection. Expected responses:

**Q (etymology):** Oromo *buna* → Arabic *qahwa* → Turkish *kahve* → Italian *caffè* → English "coffee." The trail traces trade routes: Ethiopian origin → Yemeni port → Ottoman Empire → Mediterranean commerce → European colonialism. Each linguistic transformation marks a cultural and economic exchange.

**Q (Ethiopian ceremony as narrative):** Three rounds — abol (strongest), tona (medium), baraka (lightest) — parallel rising action, development, resolution. The ceremony itself is a storytelling form; each round tells a different part of the gathering's story.

## Differentiation strategies

- **English learners:** Pronunciation guide in the master doc's Production Guide. Glossary terms appear in every data sheet.
- **Students with dyslexia:** Audio-first design supports auditory learners. Transcripts are standard.
- **Gifted students:** Bibliography gives advanced research starting points. Any project option can go 2× deeper.
- **Below grade level:** Start with Lesson 7 (quiz) for structured recall before the project.
