## About CentenarianOS

CentenarianOS is a digital health and longevity education platform. Our mission is to help people live longer, healthier lives by combining data literacy with lifestyle education. Users of the platform:

- Track personal health metrics from wearable devices (Apple Watch, Fitbit, Garmin, Oura Ring)
- Take courses taught by health coaches, fitness experts, and longevity educators
- Write and share blog posts and recipes focused on healthy aging
- Receive AI-powered insights based on their logged health data

CentenarianOS is **not a medical provider**. We are an education and self-tracking platform. All content is explicitly labeled as informational only, and users are directed to consult healthcare providers before making health decisions.

## The Course in Question: "Foundations of Fitness and Health Metrics"

Our flagship course teaches users to understand and act on the metrics their wearable devices track. The 5-week curriculum covers:

- **Week 1:** What are health metrics? The four essential metrics (Resting Heart Rate, Steps, Sleep, Activity Minutes)
- **Week 2:** Wearable device accuracy — how to interpret your data correctly
- **Week 3:** Sleep optimization — N-of-1 sleep experiments
- **Week 4:** Activity minutes and intensity — personal experiments
- **Week 5:** Building a 90-day personal health blueprint (capstone)

The course is designed for self-motivated adults who want to make data-informed health decisions. It is **not** a clinical program. Students log their own real data as part of assignments.

## Psychological Content That May Arise

Health data and self-tracking, while beneficial for many, can surface or exacerbate psychological challenges. We have identified the following content areas where emotional or mental health concerns may emerge:

### 1. Body Image and Body Weight
We have intentionally **locked body weight tracking by default** on our platform (requires explicit user acknowledgment to unlock). However, course content discussing caloric balance, body composition, and longevity may still trigger body image concerns — particularly for individuals with a history of eating disorders or disordered eating.

### 2. Anxiety About Health Numbers
Some users may develop anxiety about their metrics — obsessively tracking, feeling panic when numbers are "off," or catastrophizing normal variation in data. This can resemble health anxiety (hypochondria) or OCD-related patterns.

### 3. Discouragement and Hopelessness
Users who set health goals and don't see improvement in their metrics may experience discouragement, shame, or hopelessness. This is especially relevant for those who are older or managing chronic conditions where improvement is slower.

### 4. Sleep-Related Mental Health
Week 3 focuses extensively on sleep. Poor sleep is both a symptom and a cause of depression, anxiety, and other mental health conditions. Discussions of poor sleep data may surface distress about deeper issues.

### 5. Social Comparison
The platform includes community elements (public profiles, sharing features). Social comparison of health metrics or course achievements may affect self-esteem.

### 6. Underlying Trauma or Life Transitions
Our user base includes people navigating major life transitions (retirement, post-illness recovery, loss of a loved one, postpartum). Health optimization courses can sometimes prompt deeper reflection on mortality, identity, and meaning — which may surface grief or existential distress.

## What We Are Asking From Rise Wellness

We are requesting your clinical expertise to help us create a responsible referral and support framework. Specifically, we are requesting your guidance on:

### 1. Warning Signs — Indicators of Crisis or Concern

What behavioral or language-based indicators in a course environment (assignment text, journal entries, course messages) should prompt a concern? We want to:
- Build awareness for teachers/coaches who review student assignments
- Potentially implement an AI-assisted flagging system for crisis language in text submissions

**Questions for Rise Wellness:**
- What specific words, phrases, or themes in written submissions most reliably indicate that a student may be in crisis?
- What is the difference between "I'm having a hard time" and "I need immediate support"?
- Are there patterns in health-anxiety-related writing that your clinicians recognize?

### 2. Referral Scripts — What Should We Say?

When we surface a mental health resource (Rise Wellness contact card, 988 lifeline), we want the language to be warm, non-stigmatizing, and appropriately urgent without being alarmist.

**Questions for Rise Wellness:**
- What language do you recommend we use when presenting mental health resources to users passively (e.g., a footer note on the course page)?
- What language should we use in an active in-app banner when a concern is flagged?
- How do we frame the referral so it doesn't feel like surveillance or judgment?

### 3. Triage Criteria — What Rises to What Level?

We envision three tiers of response:

| Tier | Situation | Our Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1** | General stress, discouragement, difficulty with metrics | Passive resource card (Rise Wellness + 988 always visible on Safety page) |
| **Tier 2** | Language suggesting significant distress, hopelessness, or withdrawal | In-app banner: "We noticed you may be going through something difficult. Support is available." with Rise Wellness contact + 988 |
| **Tier 3** | Crisis language (self-harm, suicidality) | Immediate 988 prompt + encourage calling emergency services |

**Questions for Rise Wellness:**
- Does this tiered approach make clinical sense?
- What should trigger a Tier 2 vs. Tier 3 response?
- Should Tier 2 notifications go to the teacher as well as the student?

### 4. Educator/Teacher Training Guidance

Teachers on our platform grade assignments and read student submissions. They are not trained mental health professionals. We want to give them guidance on:
- What to watch for
- How to respond empathetically if a student discloses distress
- When and how to refer to the crisis resources

**Questions for Rise Wellness:**
- Can you provide a one-page guide for lay educators on recognizing and responding to student mental health disclosures?
- What should a teacher say vs. not say when a student discloses distress in an assignment?
